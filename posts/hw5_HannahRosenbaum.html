### a

### b

### c

Because Beds is correlated to Size, which is correlated to Price, so inherently, more Beds has an impact on Price but still have a low p-value.

### d

```{r}
fit <- lm(Price ~ . -Size -Beds -Baths -New, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
fit <- lm(Price ~ . -Size -Baths -New, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
fit <- lm(Price ~ . -Size -New, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
```

```{r}
fit2 <- lm(Price ~ . -New, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
fit2 <- lm(Price ~ . -New -Size, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
fit2 <- lm(Price ~ . -New -Size -Beds, data=house.selling.price)
summary(fit)
```

```{r}
summary(fit)
AIC(fit)
BIC(fit)

summary(fit2)
AIC(fit2)
BIC(fit2)
```

### e

I prefer the model generated by backward elimination, since the AIC, BIC, and multiple R-squared statistics are higher.
